About

I'm an experimental and computational geoscientist with a passion for volcanology. I investigate volcanic processes across a wide range of spatiotemporal scales in order to understand complex phenomena observed in nature. I have a background in experimental rock deformation and computational volcanology, coupled with hands-on machine engineering and programming experience. Past and current research topics include (1) Micro- to meso-scale rock deformation and fluid flow; (2) Reactive fluid flow in geologic systems; (3) Eruption triggers and dynamics; and (4) External forcing of volcanic hazards, including the impacts of climate change. I'm also a firm believer in open-access, a principle I advocate for in my roles as founder and Editor-in-Chief of Volcanica, Secretary of the Free Journal Network, and formerly as a council member of EarthArXiv. Found out more about these projects below. Check out my research interests or access my publications. I am Professor at Niigata University, Japan.

Permeability in volcanic systems

Volcanic gas release influences eruption style, magnitude, & frequency.

Permeability describes the capacity for fluids to pass through porous or granular media. In the context of volcanic activity, a high permeability can allow magmatic volatiles to escape from the system, discouraging large explosive eruptions. If, on the other hand, the system has low permeability, pressure can build up within the volcano, ultimately leading to violent explosions. By combining experimental work with numerical models and fieldwork, we can attempt to quantify how permeability evolves in volcanic systems in space and time.

Remote-sensing of Volcanic Environments

Volcanoes are often best viewed from a safe distance.

Remote-sensing platforms (ground-, aircraft-, or satellite-based, for example, can be incredibly useful tools with which to investigate dynamic volcanic environments. Some of my previous research has focussed on extracting rheological properties from emplacing rhyolite lava flows by constructing and comparing sequential digital terrain models using structure-from-motion multi-view-stereo techniques. More recently, I have been using datasets from NASA and NASA/JAXA satellite missions—in particular precipitation and SO2 emission data—in order to explore the effect of external forces on volcanic activity.

Fluid movement through deforming media

Smash it, then see what happens.

When volcanic materials are subject to mechanical stress, they can deform in different ways. Importantly, this deformation can lead to increases or decreases in porosity, associated with changes in permeability. In a series of experimental studies, I investigated the effect of brittle and ductile deformation modes on the capacity for fluid flow in volcanic rocks. Moreover, if volcanic material is above its glass transition temperature then viscous deformation such as sintering may occur. Again, this has implications for permeability and, in turn, eruption style. Image above shows a backscattered scanning electron microscope image of andesite from the Kumamoto prefecture in Kyūshū, Japan. Void space appears as black within a grey groundmass interspersed by dense, metal-rich crystals which appear as light-grey or white. This sample has been experimentally fractured and has undergone large amounts of strain—volumetric change—in order to replicate progressive mechanical deformation in a volcanic setting. Comminuted granular material is abundant within the fracture, with pulverized phenocrysts highlighting significant shear on the fault.

Chemical environments

Fire burn and caldron bubble.

Volcanoes can be pretty harsh environments—often, fluids iin volcanic environments (such as crater lakes) can have extremely low or even negative pH. Whakaari (White Island) volcano—pictured above—is a bubbling natural laboratory within which to study chemical alteration and other fluid–rock interactions, and its complex hydrothermal system has been the subject of numerous studies by the Rock Deformation Laboratory at Université de Strasbourg. A complementary study shows the influence of acid immersion on the physical and mechanical properties of andesite.

Linking laboratory and edifice-scale deformation

Upscaling measurements from sample- to volcano-scale.

Accurate inversion modelling of observed volcanic deformation signals relies on a good understanding of the mechanical behaviour of edifice-forming volcanic rocks. Ongoing research involving colleagues at University of Miami, Université de Strasbourg, and others looks at how we can effectively upscale laboratory-scale measurements to entire volcanic edifices.

Geothermal reservoirs

Harnessing Earth's power.

Previous and ongoing research investigates experimental stimulation of geothermal reservoir rocks. Under the DESTRESS Horizon-2020 framework, my project assessed the evolution of the strength and fluid-flow properties of reservoir material during acid stimulation, with a targeted focus on the Soultz-sous-Forêts geothermal plant [pictured above]. This involved designing, building, and operating a fully-customised high-temperature, high-presssure, acid permeameter at the Université de Strasbourg.

Volcanica

Making volcano research available for all.

Volcanica publishes high-quality, rigorously peer reviewed research pertaining to volcanology and related disciplines, while eliminating submission fees and keeping content freely accessible. I am proud to serve as Editor-in-Chief of Volcanica.

The website can be accessed here: https://www.jvolcanica.org.

Run by a team of dedicated volunteers, Volcanica [ISSN: 2610-3540] has published six issues of high-quality volcano-centric research since its launch in late 2017. Thanks to the enthusiastic efforts of our volunteers and an institutional sponsor—Presses universitaires de Strasbourg—we are able to ensure that the typical costs associated with publishing and accessing academic research are not passed on to the authors, the readers, their grants, or their institutions. Check out our archives here, or find out more about submitting research articles here. For more information, refer to the article below, or feel free to email me directly.

EarthArXiv

A free preprint service for the Earth sciences.

EarthArXiv is a preprint server that hosts Earth Science research. For those unfamiliar, a "preprint" is a full draft of a research paper that is shared publicly prior to formal acceptance and publication. Typically, preprints have not [yet] been formally peer reviewed. You can find out more on the EarthArXiv FAQ page. Other community resources and the EarthArXiv blog can also be found on the https://eartharxiv.github.io site. I currently serve as a member of the senior Advisory Council and moderation panel for EarthArXiv. If you are interested in hosting your preprint, be sure to check out the moderation criteria first of all.

The website can be accessed here: https://eartharxiv.org/.

Hosting your research as a preprint can have many benefits. Once they pass moderation, preprints are citable and shareable immediately. This affords authors the opportunity to receive helpful feedback from a wider pool of researchers, can help establish research primacy, and appears to result in a citation advantage relative to non-archived research articles. For more information, visit https://eartharxiv.github.io, refer to the article below, or feel free to email me directly.

Supporting open access of scholarly research.

The Free Journal Network is a nonprofit organisation dedicated to the promotion of sccholarly journals run according to the Fair Open Access model. This includes helping member journals to coordinate their efforts and share best practices, obtaining funding for member journals to make improvements to their performance, ensuring member journals continuously improve in quality, and advocating for improvements related to scholarly journal publishing more generally. More information can be found on the FJN homepage.

The website can be accessed here: https://freejournals.org/.

FJN is run by a Board of Directors and has an Advisory Board, all of whom are volunteers. Its mission is to support the ecosystem of diamond open access journals. For more information, visit https://freejournals.org/ or feel free to email me directly.

Field mode

Context is key.

It is important to consider samples, data, and models in the geological and social context of the volcano—nature’s own laboratory. Deploying monitoring apparatus, collecting samples, and performing field measurements are all important approaches for deciphering what the volcano is trying to tell us and for ensuring our research findings can be applied.

I've carried out fieldwork at Volcàn Colima, Isla Socorro, and other locations in Mexico, as well as Mount Etna (Italy), Mount Ruapehu and Whakaari (Aotearoa New Zealand). Resourcefulness and creative thinking are often essential in these volcanic areas, especially when dealing with temperamental (and/or home-made) research equipment.

Lab mode

If you want something done right cheap, do it yourself.

A great deal of my formative research experience was spent in labs and workshops in Mexico and France. At the Université de Strasbourg, I carried out measurements of rock physical and mechanical properties, supplemented with chemical and microstructural analyses. I’m an experimentalist at heart.

As well as using lab apparatus, I broke some of it and (more importantly) learnt to fix it again. I am always keen to design and build research equipment to answer existing geoscience questions, and to find new ones.

Desk mode

Keyboard warrior.

Increasingly, I leverage statistical, numerical, and data analytics approaches to help explain field observations and laboratory measurements. Especially the weird ones.

Data is at its best when it is effectively visualised. A key aim of mine is to ensure that data is accessible in all its forms, and that includes on the page.

Climate, weather, and volcanic hazard

Exploring the links between the hydrosphere and geosphere.

It is known that volcanic eruptions can exert changes in Earth's climate, largely due to the radiative and chemical effects of erupted gases and aerosols. However, the inverse scenario—the influence of climate and extreme weather on volcanism—is understudied. Underlying these complex interconnections becomes increasingly relevant in light of ongoing rapid climate change.

Dynamics of silicic eruptions

Deciphering Earth's rarest eruptions.

Understanding the controls on silicic volcanic eruption style is a grand challenge in geoscience. Silicic eruptions—and rhyolitic eruptions in particular—remain enigmatic, in part because very few such eruptions have been observed.
